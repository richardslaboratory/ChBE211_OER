Liquefaction#

import os
from IPython.display import Image
path = 'C:\\Users\\richa\\OneDrive - Northwestern University\\Northwestern\\Teaching\\Thermodynamics Notes\\Thermodynamics-Notes\\images'

Liquefaction is the process of making liquids from gases that do not condense under normal circumstances. It is desirable to make liquids in many cases because liquids are more dense their vapor counterparts, making them less-expensive to transport. Liquid Natural Gas, butane, and other commodity liquids are manufactured and stored as liquids.

The easiest way to form a liquid from a volatile substance is to compress its vapor into a high pressure vapor and expand it across a valve into a flash drum. A flash drum is a unit operation that operates adiabatically and allows a vapor and liquid in equilibrium to be separated as depicted below:

Image(os.path.join(path, "Liquefaction.png"))

Mass, Energy, and Entropy balance for a flash drum.#

\((1)\quad \dot{N}_2 + \dot{N}_3 + \dot{N}_4 = 0\)

\((2)\quad (\dot{N}\underline{H})_2 + (\dot{N}\underline{H})_3 + (\dot{N}\underline{H}))_4 = 0\)

\((3)\quad (\dot{N}\underline{S})_2 + (\dot{N}\underline{S})_3 + (\dot{N}\underline{S}))_4 + \dot{S}_{gen}= 0\)

With the constraint that the fluid and vapor streams exiting (3) and (4) respectively are saturated at a constant pressure.

Recall that we can derive the molar liquid fraction, \(x_l\) and molar vapor fraction, \(x_v\) using the tie line or the lever rule.

\[x_v = \dfrac{\dot{N}_4}{\dot{N_2}} = \dfrac{\underline{H}_2 - \underline{H}_3}{\underline{H}_4 - \underline{H}_3}\]

and

\[x_v = 1 - x_l\]

where \(\underline{H}_3\) is the enthalpy of the saturated liquid and \(\underline{H}_4\) is the enthalpy of the saturated vapor exiting the flash drum.

Pressure-Enthalpy Diagram:#

As shown in the figure above, in order to expand across a valve and generate a liquid, the gas must be pressurized and cooled to a position above the 2-phase boundary (depicted in the P-H diagram). This superheated and cooled vapor is then passed through a valve where it expands and generates a 2-phase mixture. The phases are collected in a flash drum at a constant pressure and it is often the case that the vapor phase is recycled back into the process and the liquid phase collected and stored.

Simple Liquefaction:#

In a simple liquefaction scheme, a series of staged compression/cooling cycles are performed to raise the pressure of the gas above its critical pressure. It is then finally cooled isobarically to reach the desired temperature prior to expansion as defined by the \(P-\underline{H}\) diagram. The key idea for the cooling process is that expansion across a valve will be isoenthalpic resulting in a vertical cut on the \(P-\underline{H}\) diagram depicted in the cartoon from state points 3-4. The result will be the formation of a mixture of a saturated liquid (5) and saturated vapor (6). In liquefaction the saturated liquid is your product and the low pressure vapor can be recycled back into the process or exhausted to atmosphere in the case of liquifying air.

The compression and cooling requirements may require multiple stages to reach the necessary pressure to liquify across the value. These multiple stages will result in a series of shark-fin shaped curves that raise the pressure without exceeding a maximum temperature within the compressor as shown below from state points (1)-(2).

Image(os.path.join(path, "Simple Liquefaction.png"))

Illustration 5.1-1 in Sandler illustrates this nicely for the liquefaction of Methane. Reproducing that example here#

It is desired to produce liquified natural gas (LNG), where pure methane is available at \(1\) \(bar\) and \(280\) \(K\). Leaving the cooler, methane is at \(100\) \(bar\) and \(210\) \(K\). The flash drum is adiabatic and operates at 1 bar, and the compressor can be assumed to operate reversibly and adiabatically. However, because of the large pressure change, a three-stage compression with intercooling is used. The first stage compresses the gas from \(1\) to \(5\) \(bar\), the second stage from \(5\) to \(25\) \(bar\) and the third stage from \(25\) \(bar\) to \(100\) \(bar\). Between the stages the gas is isobarically cooled to \(280\) \(K\).

The astericks in the table above denote the state points given in the problem statement. The apostrophes denote the different stage compressors and coolers. By identifying the values in the table associate with the problem statement, the remaining values can be determined graphically.

Image(os.path.join(path, "methane_P-Hdiagram.png"))

Once we have the values filled out in the table, then we can add up the work of compression as follows:

\(\dfrac{\dot{W}_{1-2}}{\dot{M}} = \hat{H}_2 - \hat{H}_1 = 1150 - 870 = 280 kJ/kg\)

\(\dfrac{\dot{W}_{1'-2'}}{\dot{M}} = \hat{H}_2' - \hat{H}_1' = 1150 - 865 = 285 kJ/kg\)

\(\dfrac{\dot{W}_{1''-2''}}{\dot{M}} = \hat{H}_2'' - \hat{H}_1'' = 1100 - 855 = 240 kJ/kg\)

The sum is then

\(\dfrac{\dot{W}_{1-2''}}{\dot{M}} = 280 + 285 + 240 = 805\) \(kJ/kg\)

b.) Calculate the vapor and liquid fractions leaving the flash drum and the amount of compressor work for each kg of LNG produced.#

For this, we use the lever rule:

\[w_v = \dfrac{\underline{H}_4 - \underline{H}_5}{\underline{H}_6 - \underline{H}_5} = \dfrac{420}{510} = 0.82 \]

$\(w_l = 1 - w_v = 0.18\)

To get the work per unit LNG produced:

\(\dfrac{\dot{W}_{1-2''}}{w_l \dot{M}} = 805 / 0.12 = 4,472\) \(kJ/kg\)

c.) Assuming the recycled methane leaving the heat exchange in the Linde Process (shown below) is at \(1\) \(bar\) and \(200\) \(K\), calculate the amount of compressor work required for each kilogram of LNG produced#

Image(os.path.join(path, "Linde Liquefaction.png"))

Examining the Linde process, there are a few notable advantages compred to simple liquefaction.

  1. The low pressure and cold methane can be recycled back into the process allowing for the displacement of significant cooling needs after the last stage of the compressor reaches its final pressure.

  2. Methane is a potent greenhouse gas so recycling it back into the process allows for the elimination of most emissions associated with its liquefaction

  3. Analyzing the work requirements in the compressor, we find that 1’ has a significantly colder temperature than the methane was available (\(280\) \(K\)) of \(233\) \(K\). This significantly reduces the amount of work required per unit kg of LNG produced. \(1641\) vs \(4149\) \(kJ/kg\) for the simple liquefaction process.
