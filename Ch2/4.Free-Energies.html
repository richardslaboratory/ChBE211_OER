Free Energy#

The free energy is the energy available to do useful work. You probably learned about free energy in General Chemistry in the context of chemical reactions. In that context, exothermic reactions are reactions where the free energy change from the initial to final state is negative and endothermic processes are those where the free energy change is positive.

In thermodynamics, we consider how the free energy is affected not by chemical processes but instead by physical ones.

Free Energy Definition - The Combination of 1st and 2nd Laws:#

It is convenient to define the free energies by beginning with the 1st and 2nd laws of thermodynamics. We have shown in previous lectures that the mass, energy, and entropy balance expressions can be written on a molar basis in the differential form as:

  1. \(\dfrac{dN}{dt}=\sum_k\dot{N}_k\)

  2. \(\dfrac{dU}{dt}=\sum_k\big(\dot{N}\underline{H}\big)_k+\dot{Q}-P_{ext}\dfrac{dV}{dt}+\dot{W}\)

  3. \(\dfrac{dS}{dt}=\sum_k\big(\dot{N}\underline{S}\big)_k+\dfrac{\dot{Q}}{T}+\dot{S}_{gen}\)

If we combine (2) and (3), we can show that we get:

rearranging, we get

  • \(\dfrac{dU}{dt}=\sum_k\big(\dot{N}(\underline{H}-T\underline{S}\big)_k+T\dfrac{dS}{dt}-P_{ext}\dfrac{dV}{dt}-T\dot{S}_{gen}+\dot{W}\)

Two useful cases emerge from this mess:

  1. Open System Problems at Steady State

  2. Closed Systems with Moving Boundaries

Gibb’s Free Energy#

Open Systems at Steady State#

For an open system operating at steady state with fixed boundaries, we can write.

\(\quad 0=\sum_k\big(\dot{N}(\underline{H}-T\underline{S}\big)_k-T\dot{S}_{gen}+\dot{W}\)

If we define the Gibb’s Free Energy as \(G=H-TS\), then the above can be simplified as:

\(\quad 0=\sum_k\big(\dot{N}\underline{G}\big)_k-T\dot{S}_{gen}+\dot{W}\)

Conceptual Meaning of Gibb’s Free Energy#

The form of the equation above explicitly links work and entropy generation to the change in molar free energy of a vapor/liquid as it undergoes some physical process. We can simplify things further for a single mass flow, \(\dot{N}\). From (1), we know that \(\dot{N}_2=-\dot{N}\) and therefore, we can write

\(\quad 0=\dot{N}\big(\underline{G}_2-\underline{G}_1)-T\dot{S}_{gen}+\dot{W}\)

This form of the equation is the most intuitive. If you remember anything from this class, remember these three maxims

  • Work done by the system \(\dot{W}<0\) on the surroundings causes the a decrease in the Gibb’s Free Energy, \(\underline{G}_2<\underline{G}_1\), of the fluid.

  • Work done on the system \(\dot{W}>0\) by the surroundings causes the an increase in the Gibb’s Free Energy, \(\underline{G}_2>\underline{G}_1\), of the fluid.

  • Entropy generation is always positive, \(\dot{S}_{gen}>0\), therefore the interconversion of work to free energy and visa versa is always incomplete.

    • When work is done on on the system by the surroundings, this will result in a smaller increase in Gibb’s Free Energy than for the reversible process.

    • When work is done by on the system by the surroundings, this will result in less work output for an equivalent change in Gibb’s Free Energy than for the reversible process.

We can imagine cyclically running a fluid through a compressor and turbine. The implication of the statements above is that through each cycle of compression and expansion, additional work must be supplied to make up for the accumulating losses associated with entropy generation. Therefore, perpetual motion machines that operate at steady state are impossible to construct and violate the 2nd Law of thermodynamics.

Helmholtz Free Energy#

Closed Systems with Moving Boundaries#

For a closed system that has moving boundaries, we can group the time derivative terms on the L.H.S

  • \(\dfrac{d}{dt}\bigg(U-TS\bigg)=-P_{ext}\dfrac{dV}{dt}-T\dot{S}_{gen}\)

If we define the Helmholtz Free Energy as \(A=U-TS\), then the above can be simplified as:

  • \(\dfrac{dA}{dt}=-P_{ext}\dfrac{dV}{dt}-T\dot{S}_{gen}\)’

Conceptual Meaning of the “Helmholtz Free Energy”#

The form of the equation above explicitly links work and entropy generation to the change in extrinsic free energy of a vapor/liquid as it undergoes compression/expansion in a closed volume. Integrating both sides of this expression, we find that:

\(\quad A_2-A_1=-P_{ext}\big(\Delta V) - \int_{1}^{2}T\dot{S}_{gen}dt\)

where

\(\quad \Delta V = V_{2}-V_{1}\)

This form of the equation is the most intuitive. If you remember anything from this class, remember these three maxims

  • Work of expansion done on the surroundings \(\Delta V>0\) by the system causes the a decrease in the Helmholtz Free Energy, \(A_2<A_1\), of the fluid.

  • Work of compression done by the surroundings \(\Delta V< 0\) on the system causes the an increase in the Helmholtz Free Energy, \(A_2>A_1\), of the fluid.

  • Entropy generation is always positive, \(\dot{S}_{gen}\ge 0\), therefore the interconversion of boundary work to free energy and visa versa is always incomplete.

    • When work is done on on the system by the surroundings, this will result in a smaller increase in Helmholtz Free Energy than for the reversible process.

    • When work is done by on the system by the surroundings, this will result in less work output for an equivalent change in Helmholtz Free Energy than for the reversible process.

We can imagine a sealed piston cyclically compressing and expanding a fluid. The implication of the statements above is that through each cycle of compression and expansion, additional work must be supplied to make up for the accumulating losses associated with entropy generation. Therefore, perpetual motion machines that operate at steady state are impossible to construct and violate the 2nd Law of thermodynamics.
