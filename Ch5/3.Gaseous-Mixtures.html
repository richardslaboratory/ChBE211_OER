Gaseous Mixtures#

Gaseous mixtures are generally considered to be easier to quantitatively predict the state behavior as compared with liquid mixtures. This is because gases are generally more expanded than liquids. Therefore, their interactions are generally less frequent than in liquids and of less consequence to their material properties.

While there are many quantitative theories that seek to describe the state of a mixture at a fixed temperature and pressure as a function of composition, we will focus on two methods:

  1. Ideal Gas Mixtures

  2. Cubic Mixture Equations of State

Ideal Gas Mixtures#

Ideal gas mixtures are mixtures which obey the ideal gas law which for the purposes of this discussion, we write as:

\[PV=NRT\]

An ideal gas mixture is a mixture where the gas molecules are so expanded that they neither interact with other species in the mixture nor themselves. We can compute the properties of an ideal gas mixture by assuming that each species in the mixture contributes to to the total pressure proportionally to the number of moles of that speices or:

\[PV=RT\sum_i^C N_i\]

In addition to obeying the ideal gas law, ideal gas mixtures have a number of important properties.

  1. \(\bar{V}_i=\underline{V}_i\)

  2. \(\bar{U}_i=\underline{U}_i\)

  3. \(\bar{H}_i=\underline{H}_i\)

Another important property of ideal gas mixtures is their Partial Pressure, \(P_i\) is given as the product of the total pressure and the mole fraction (this can be derived from eq (ref needed) above). Mathematically, we say that

\[P_i=y_iP=\dfrac{N_iRT}{V}\]
Note: To denote mole fraction of a gaseous species, it is tradition to use y instead of x.

Because there is no difference in internal energy or density of an ideal gas containing \(N\) moles of an ideal gas versus \(N=\sum_i N_i\) moles of mixture species, it is tempting to think that the formation of an ideal gas mixture has no energetic conequence. However, where ideal gas mixtures become distinguishable from ideal gases is their Entropy.

The entropy change upon forming an ideal gas mixture is given as:

\[\Delta_{mix}\underline{S}=-R\sum_i^{C}y_i\ln{y_i}\]

This equation, sometimes called the Stirling Approximation resembles the [multiplicity equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicity_(statistical_mechanics) given by Boltzmann. This is not an accident. What this equation states is that ideal gas mixtures are mixtures where the species are thoroughly mixed such that all atoms in the mixture have an equal likelihood to be present within the volume.

For based on the above, the thermal properties of Ideal Gas Mixtures can be summarized:

  • \(\underline{U}^{IGM}(T,\underline{x})=\sum_i y_i\underline{U}_i^{IG}(T)\)

  • \(\underline{V}^{IGM}(T,P,\underline{x})=\sum_i y_i\underline{V}_i^{IG}(T,P)\)

  • \(\underline{H}^{IGM}(T,P,\underline{x})=\sum_i y_i\underline{H}_i^{IG}(T,P)\)

  • \(P(T,\underline{V})=\sum_i P_i(T,V)\)

  • \(\underline{S}^{IGM}(T,P,\underline{x})=\sum_i y_i\underline{S}_i^{IG}(T,P)-R\sum_i y_i \ln{y_i}\)

Non-ideal Gas Mixtures#

At higher pressures and densities, significant deviation from Ideal Gas Mixture behavior is frequently encountered. Just like with single component fluids, these scenarios are treated using virial expansions of the equation of state, but modified for mixtures.

Virial Equation of State for Mixtures#

At low pressures, the 2nd-order virial expansion proves to be a good approximation:

\[\dfrac{P\underline{V}}{RT}=1+\dfrac{B_{mix}(T,\underline{y})}{\underline{V}}\]

This equation is identical in form to the single component expression except now \(B_{mix}\) depends on the composition of the mixture. Various mixing rules are used to describe the composition dependence of \(B_{mix}\) but from statistical mechanics, we have:

\[B_{mix}(T,\underline{y})=\sum_i\sum_j y_i y_j B_{ij}(T)\]

where \(B_{ij}=B_{ji}\)

For a Binary Mixture this simplifies to:

\[B_{mix}(T,\underline{y})=y_1^2B_1(T)+2y_1 y_2 B_{12}(T)+y_2^2B_2(T)\]

We can see that by using this simple mixing rule, a new parameter has appeared, \(B_{12}(T)\), which is a property unique to a given mixture.

Note: Virial coefficients for many mixtures can be found in the book The Virial Coefficients of Pure Gases and Mixtures: A Critical Compilation by J. H. Dymond

Cubic Equations of State for Mixtures#

At higher pressures, the cubic equations of state must be used. Similar to the Virial Expansion, the cubic equations of state retain their same functional form with new mixing rules that describe the attractive and repulsive interaction parameters \(a\) and \(b\). As an example, we can write the mixture Peng-Robinson Equation of state as:

\[P=\dfrac{RT}{\underline{V}-b_{mix}}-\dfrac{a_{mix}(T)}{\underline{V}(\underline{V}-b_{mix})+b_{mix}(\underline{V}-b_{mix})}\]

using the following mixing rules:

  1. \(a_{mix}=\sum_i\sum_j y_i y_j a_{ij}\)

where \(a_{ij}=\sqrt{a_ii a_jj}(1-k_{ij})=a_{ji}\) and \(k_{ij}\) is a dimensionless quantity defined as the binary interaction parameter that is unique for each pair in the mixture.

  1. \(b_{mix}=\sum_i y_i b_i\)
